1. NUS engineers unveil compact, power-holding radio-frequency switches for 5G and future 6G chips

A team led by Associate Professor Mario Lanza at the NUS College of Design and Engineering built a new type of memristive radio-frequency switch from a wafer-thin hexagonal boron nitride layer bonded to gallium-nitride microchips, and used it to reconfigure working high-frequency circuits for the first time. Each switch measures just 2 by 2 micrometres — roughly 25,000 times smaller than conventional switches — and, crucially, retains its setting without continuous power, cutting the energy overhead as devices pack in the 50-plus switches that 5G and 6G hardware require. The team demonstrated the switches inside three working circuits (an attenuator, a power divider, and a filter) and reported the work in Nature on 8 July 2026, though they note more work is needed on manufacturing complexity and cycle durability before commercial use. Coverage frames it as a marquee win for NUS engineering research, reinforcing the university's reputation in materials science and telecoms hardware.

Sources: EurekAlert, Nature (paper)

2. NUS mathematicians develop AI that learns the hidden rules governing how materials behave at scale

A team led by Associate Professor Qianxiao Li in the NUS Department of Mathematics has built AI methods that infer the large-scale behaviour of complex materials — from alloys to fluids and polymers — directly from small-scale microscopic simulations, sidestepping the prohibitively expensive atom-by-atom modelling that usually limits materials research. One breakthrough, validated on simulated alloys of over 500,000 atoms, lets researchers extrapolate accurate large-system behaviour from data on a small fraction of particles; the second lets the AI handle systems where particles have no natural ordering, such as fluids. The work, published in Physical Review Materials and presented at ICML 2026, is pitched as a step toward faster, cheaper discovery of new materials for energy, electronics and manufacturing. It's a second NUS research story surfacing the same week, adding to a run of positive research-prestige coverage for the university.

Sources: NUS Faculty of Science, Tech Xplore

3. UOB partners NUS's Centre for Quantum Technologies to pioneer quantum computing for derivatives pricing

UOB is collaborating with NUS's Centre for Quantum Technologies (CQT), supported by the National Quantum Computing Hub, to explore whether quantum-computing methods can price complex financial derivatives faster and more accurately than today's Monte Carlo simulations. The initial phase targets path-dependent instruments — among the most computationally demanding to value — with CQT principal investigator and NUS computer science associate professor Patrick Rebentrost developing "beyond-Monte-Carlo" quantum techniques to test against real-world scenarios. The tie-up positions NUS as a hub bridging academic quantum research and practical banking applications, and follows Singapore's 2024 National Quantum Strategy, under which CQT and the National Quantum Computing Hub both operate. It's a reputational win that ties NUS research directly to the local financial sector.

Sources: Frontier Enterprise

4. Law firm WongPartnership endows S$1 million NUS Law professorship honouring late senior partner Alvin Yeo

WongPartnership LLP announced a S$1,000,000 gift to NUS establishing the Alvin Yeo WongPartnership Visiting Professorship at the Faculty of Law, commemorating co-founder Alvin Yeo, Senior Counsel, who died in 2022 and was, at 37, the youngest lawyer ever appointed Senior Counsel in Singapore. The professorship will bring a distinguished scholar or practitioner in commercial law and dispute resolution to NUS Law each year to teach, research and deliver a public lecture, with the first appointee expected in the 2026/2027 academic year. NUS Law Dean Professor Andrew Simester welcomed the gift as reinforcing a partnership dating back to a 2009 scholarship. Coming the same week as other prestige-building news, it adds to a pattern of major Singapore institutions channelling philanthropy toward NUS.

Sources: WongPartnership
